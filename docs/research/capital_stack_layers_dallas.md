## 1. Tax Increment Financing (TIF) Districts

### National Context

**Structural Design Critique (Good Jobs First)**

Tax Increment Financing is active in every U.S. state except Arizona. Texas has the third-highest TIF count in the nation at 1,378 districts, behind only Iowa (3,340) and Minnesota (1,719). ([Good Jobs First, "What is Tax Increment Financing?" January 2025](https://goodjobsfirst.org/tax-increment-financing/); [Good Jobs First TIF FAQ, 2022](https://goodjobsfirst.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/03/TIF-FAQs-.pdf))

Key structural critiques from Good Jobs First:
- **The "but for" problem:** State legislation enabling TIF rarely requires community benefits for incumbent district residents. Critics document that many TIF-subsidized projects would likely have proceeded anyway — meaning TIF often shifts development within a region rather than creating net new economic growth.
- **School fund diversion:** In a typical property tax revenue split, half may go to schools and half to city/county. TIF captures *all* incremental streams and pours them into a small area for decades — diverting revenue that would otherwise fund schools, fire departments, and public health clinics.
- **Geographic favoring of wealthy areas:** Good Jobs First documented in the Twin Cities that three-fourths of TIF subsidy deals involving corporate relocations moved jobs *away from* communities of color, away from public transit routes, and away from pockets of poverty — into wealthier communities. "Geographically, the TIF deals favor the outer parts of the region."
- **Chicago case study:** In 2019, TIF diverted $900 million in property tax revenue from Chicago's city government, public schools, and other local taxing authorities, primarily for development projects in wealthier areas. Of the nine TIF districts that generated the most revenue that year, eight were in downtown or nearby areas. ([Pew Charitable Trusts, "How States Can Direct Economic Development," February 2021](https://www.pew.org/en/research-and-analysis/reports/2021/02/how-states-can-direct-economic-development-to-places-and-people-in-need))

**Academic Research (UC Berkeley Terner Center)**

The Terner Center's analysis ("The Perils and Promises of Redevelopment," 2023) confirms TIF is one tool cities use to incentivize private sector development by "capturing" increases in assessed property values. The fundamental critique: TIF revenue is supposed to be directed to private developers only when real estate appreciates — but cities frequently front capital costs on behalf of developers regardless. ([UC Berkeley Terner Center, 2023](https://ternercenter.berkeley.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/06/The-Perils-and-Promises-of-Redevelopment_FINAL_Hart.pdf))

The creation of the Vickery Meadow TIF District in 2006 paved the way for mixed-use developments like The Shops at Park Lane — where **no units were reserved for affordable housing**. Legacy immigrant residents who had previously been displaced from their home countries now faced a second displacement. Only in Vickery Meadow's oldest apartment homes can one still find a studio unit for under $1,000 per month. ([CandysDirt.com, "What Dallas Neighborhoods Are Vulnerable to Gentrification?" **Southern Dallas Context from City Policy Documents**

The City of Dallas's own 2021 Economic Development Policy acknowledges the structural imbalance: **Southern Dallas (below I-30/Trinity River) encompasses only 15% of the tax base but is home to 45% of the population.** Research cited by the Dallas Regional Chamber identifies an even wider gap: a 2022 white paper found that nine southern council districts, containing roughly **64% of the city's population**, hold only **10% of its total property value**. ([City of Dallas Economic Development Policy 2022–2032, May 2021](https://content.govdelivery.com/attachments/TXDALLAS/2021/05/26/file_attachments/1834960/CITY%20OF%20DALLAS%20ECONOMIC%20DEVELOPMENT%20POLICY.pdf); [D Magazine, "The Tale of Dallas' Inequities," May 2022](https://www.dmagazine.com/frontburner/2022/05/the-tale-of-dallas-inequities-can-be-told-with-these-two-maps/))

**Small Business Capital Data for South Dallas**

From Dallas's Small Business Ecosystem Assessment: South Dallas includes only 11 of the 529 census tracts in Dallas County but includes 40% of the lowest income census tracts. ZIP codes 75210 and 75215 are the 3rd and 6th lowest income ZIP codes in Dallas County. In 2017, **2.8x more capital was deployed to small businesses per capita in Dallas County than in South Dallas**. Only 220 bank loans were made in South Dallas in 2017, of which only 18 exceeded $100,000. South Dallas has only four FDIC-insured bank branches. ([Dallas Office of Economic Development, Small Business Ecosystem Assessment](https://www.dallasecodev.org/DocumentCenter/View/2789/Dallas-Small-Business-Ecosystem-Report-PDF))

## 2. Opportunity Zones (OZ)

### Program Background

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 designated 8,764 census tracts as Opportunity Zones, offering investors capital gains tax deferral, step-up in basis, and exclusion from taxation on appreciation for 10+ year investments. Governors selected which eligible tracts to nominate. More than **$100 billion** has been invested in OZs since 2018. 75% of that investment through 2022 went to **real estate**, most of it residential. ([Brookings Institution, "How did the One Big Beautiful Bill Act change Opportunity Zones?" July 2025](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/how-did-the-one-big-beautiful-bill-act-change-opportunity-zones/))

In July 2025, the One Big Beautiful Bill Act made the OZ program permanent, sunsetting the current round at end of 2026 and creating new designations beginning January 2027.

---

### Investment Flow: Gentrifying vs. Distressed Tracts

**Treasury/IRS Data (Coyne, Johnson, Corinth, Feldman — 2022 data)**

Using tax return data through 2022, Treasury economists and co-authors found that about **two-thirds of designated OZ tracts received some OZ investment**, but:

> "OZ investment tends to flow to census tracts with greater pre-existing private investment and other conditions reflective of strong demand, within prosperous counties, and in areas of the country experiencing greater growth."

Key geographic findings:
- **93% of all OZ investment went to metropolitan areas** — rural areas, which comprised the program's equity rationale, received almost nothing.
- States receiving the *most* OZ investment per capita: Wyoming, Utah, Arizona, Nevada, and Washington, D.C.
- **75% of OZ investment went to zones with the top 20% of commercial investment** — i.e., the already most-developed zones.
- More OZ investment went to zones in counties with higher incomes and educational attainment. ([Urban Institute, "Opportunity Zones Need to Be Retooled," May 2025](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/opportunity-zones-need-be-retooled-achieve-impact))

**Urban Institute — Ohio Case Study (2025)**

An Urban Institute study of Ohio's OZ program (tracts from 2020 to 2024) found:
- OZ investment was **concentrated in a few zones** — 60% of funds went to only 2% of designated and funded tracts.
- Tracts receiving OZ investment had, *before the program began*, experienced: home price increases of 16%, median rent increases of 13%, median household income increases of 30%, and a 50% increase in college-educated residents. These were **already-appreciating neighborhoods**.
- **78% of OZ-funded residential units in Ohio** were priced above local median rents. Nearly 70% were priced above 120% of tract median rent.
- Conclusion: "The OZ program functions more as a real estate incentive than a tool for inclusive development." ([Urban Institute, "The Geographic Spread of Opportunity Zone Capital," October 2025](https://www.urban.org/research/publication/insights-opportunity-zone-project-types))

**NLIHC / Brookings — Gentrifying Tracts and OZ Investment**

Research by Kurban, Otabor, and Cole-Smith (presented at Brookings, February 2021) found:
- On average, OZ-designated tracts had *lower* gentrification scores (1.49) compared to eligible but non-designated tracts (1.91) — meaning governors did not systematically select the most gentrifying areas.
- **However**, within OZ-designated tracts, **gentrifying tracts are significantly more likely to receive investment** than non-gentrifying OZ tracts.
- In Washington, D.C.: 68% of OZ-designated census tracts were **either gentrifying or adjacent to two or more gentrifying tracts**.
- In D.C., gentrifying OZ tracts showed: higher in-migration of higher-income residents, displacement of lower-income residents, and a higher number of construction permits. ([NLIHC, "Gentrifying Opportunity Zones Are More Likely than Non-Gentrifying OZs to Receive Investment," April 2022](https://nlihc.org/resource/gentrifying-opportunity-zones-are-more-likely-non-gentrifying-opportunity-zones-receive); [Brookings, "Was gentrification a factor in designation of Opportunity Zones," presentation slides, February 2021](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2021/01/Kurban-et-al_slides.pdf))

**NCRC — OZ and Gentrification (2020)**

The National Community Reinvestment Coalition's 2020 analysis of OZs found:
- Nearly **84% of urban OZs overlapped with CRA-designated low-to-moderate income neighborhoods** — confirming geographic targeting of distressed areas at the designation stage.
- However, **almost 69% of neighborhoods identified as gentrifying (2013–2017 data) were either within or adjacent to an OZ**.
- OZ tracts saw deteriorating median home values and decreasing college-educated residents — indicators that investment had not yet reached deeply distressed zones.
- Conclusion: "A study focus for OZs should be placed on the types and quality of investments attracted to them, and whether these investments contribute to the economic well-being of low-income residents." ([NCRC, "Gentrification and Disinvestment 2020," June 2020](https://ncrc.org/gentrification20/))

**Key Pattern:** Governors selected distressed areas on average, but capital investors within those designated zones self-select the already-improving tracts — a two-stage filter that ensures the deepest poverty tracts receive the least capital.

**Port Covington (Baltimore) as Documented Abuse Case**

The NLIHC's 2023 Advocates' Guide documents a specific case of OZ manipulation: Port Covington in Baltimore, which already had $660 million in TIF financing, a Brownfields tax credit, and $233 million from Goldman Sachs, was too wealthy to qualify as an OZ on its own. Through intensive lobbying and a mapping error, it was designated anyway. ([NLIHC, "Opportunity Zones," 2023 Advocates' Guide](https://nlihc.org/sites/default/files/2023-03/2023AG9-03_Opportunity-Zones.pdf))

## 3. Public Improvement Districts (PIDs) and HOAs in Dallas

### What PIDs Are and How They Function

A Public Improvement District (PID) is a special assessment area created at the request of property owners. Dallas City policy allows PIDs to levy a maximum assessment of **$0.15 per $100 of property valuation**, with the City collecting assessments and returning them to a designated nonprofit management entity for services. Services include: enhanced security patrols, landscaping, sanitation, marketing, and capital improvements — services that supplement (not replace) regular city services. ([City of Dallas Office of Economic Development, PIDs page](https://www.dallasecodev.org/245/Public-Improvement-Districts); [Dallas City Hall, PID Overview PDF, August 2016](https://dallascityhall.com/government/Council%20Meeting%20Documents/eco_2_public-improvement-districts-pids-overview_combined_081516.pdf))

### Current Dallas PIDs (15 Active as of 2025–2026)

The Dallas City Council approved 2026 service plans for 15 PIDs in August 2025, including two new additions (RedBird and Far East Dallas). ([Dallas Office of Economic Development, PID Service Plans Approved, August 2025](https://www.dallasecodev.org/CivicAlerts.asp?AID=1163&ARC=1295))

### Geographic Concentration Analysis: North vs. South Dallas

**The Assessment Revenue Gap**

The geographic pattern reveals a stark North–South divide in PID revenue generation:

- The **Uptown PID** alone generates **$4.17 million** in supplemental revenue at a *low* rate of $0.045/\$100 — reflecting the extremely high property values in Uptown.
- The **South Side PID** (serving neighborhoods south of downtown) generates only **$411,526** — less than 10% of Uptown's revenue.
- The **RedBird PID** (serving the southwest, near South Dallas) generates only **$320,391**.
- Together, the two explicitly south/southwest PIDs (South Side + RedBird) generate a combined **$731,917** — compared to **$13.49 million** for the Downtown Improvement District alone.

**Uptown PID Property Value Context**

From the 2016 PID Overview document: the Uptown PID had total assessed value of over $5 billion (inferred from South Side PID comparison with 45 property owners vs. 2,333 in Vickery Meadow), with the highest year-over-year valuation growth rate among all Dallas PIDs at 15.9% (2015–2016). ([Dallas City Hall, PID Overview, 2016](https://dallascityhall.com/government/Council%20Meeting%20Documents/eco_2_public-improvement-districts-pids-overview_combined_081516.pdf))

**How PID Assessments Function as a Layered Private Tax**

PIDs impose a supplemental property tax on all property owners within their boundaries, redirected entirely to district-specific services. This creates a two-tier service geography:

1. **North Dallas / Uptown / Downtown:** Multiple overlapping PIDs fund 24/7 private security patrols, professional landscaping, marketing, special events, and capital improvements — effectively privatizing and enhancing the public realm far beyond what the general city budget provides.
2. **South Dallas:** Almost no PID coverage exists. The South Dallas/Fair Park PID (noted in the 2016 document as an exception to standard petition threshold rules due to community organizing) had just one year of assessment at the time and generated no comparable revenue.

**The Sunny South Dallas PID Effort (2025–2026)**

Community developer Scottie Smith II has been attempting to organize a "Sunny South Dallas PID" covering roughly **1,375 parcels** with a total property value of **$375.95 million** and 7 million square feet. The estimated generation would be approximately **$9 million over the next decade** — substantially less than a single year of assessment in the Downtown Improvement District ($13.5 million). ([Dallas Free Press, "Sunny South Dallas PID Explained," December 2025](https://dallasfreepress.com/south-dallas/how-dallas-free-press-is-covering-the-proposed-sunny-south-dallas-pid/); [Preston Hollow Press, "Sunny South Dallas PID: Can a New District Succeed?" March 2026](https://prestonhollowpress.com/news/sunny-south-dallas-pid-can-a-new-district-succeed))

**Research Context: BIDs and Power Concentration**

UC Berkeley's Goldman School (2019) found that Business Improvement Districts (BIDs — equivalent to PIDs) are associated with increases in residential rents and housing development. More critically, "BID governance concentrates decision-making power with property owners that prioritize their interests and are often not representative of the community." The voices excluded from BID boards are residents, community members, and business owners without property. In case studies, the voices of community members who are "disproportionately low-income people of color, are absent from the decision-making process." ([UC Berkeley Goldman School of Public Policy, "Business Improvement Districts and Their Impact on Communities," May 2019](https://www.law.berkeley.edu/wp-content/uploads/2019/07/Goldman-School-BIDS-Report-May-2019.pdf))

## 4. Institutional Investor / Corporate Landlord Concentration

### Dallas-Fort Worth Single-Family Rental (SFR) Market Overview

Dallas-Fort Worth has the sixth-largest concentration of institutional single-family rental ownership among major U.S. metros:

- **Total mega investor-owned properties in DFW:** 26,961 (as of 2022)
- **Mega investor share of all rental properties:** 2.37%
- **Mega investor share of SFR properties:** 8.31%
- **Total rental units in DFW:** 1,329,920
- **Single-family share of all rentals:** 28.41%

*Source: [Urban Institute/Brookings, "Single-family rentals: Trends and policy recommendations," November 2023](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/20231102_THP_SingleFamilyRentals_Proposal.pdf)*

### Racial and Geographic Concentration

**Urban Institute Profile of Investor-Owned SFR Properties (April 2023)**

Using data from the top 20 institutional investor MSAs, the Urban Institute found for Dallas-Fort Worth:

| Metric | All Rental Households (DFW) | All Institutional (DFW) | Mega Investors (DFW) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nonwhite renter share | 59.5% | 52.2% | 52.8% |
| **Black renter share** | 26.3% | **28.9%** | **30.0%** |
| Latino renter share | 25.1% | 18.6% | 18.1% |
| Median household income (tracts) | $49,757 | $59,873 | $60,133 |

**Key finding:** Mega investors in Dallas concentrate in census tracts with **higher Black renter shares than the overall MSA rental average** (30.0% vs. 26.3%) but **higher median household income** ($60,133 vs. $49,757). This reflects a targeting of "strike zone" neighborhoods — historically Black areas that are now appreciating or subject to gentrification pressure, where homes are cheaper than surrounding areas but offer strong rental income. ([Urban Institute, "A Profile of Institutional Investor-Owned Single-Family Rental Properties," April 2023](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2023-04/A%20Profile%20of%20Institutional%20Investor%E2%80%93Owned%20Single-Family%20Rental%20Properties.pdf))

**National Pattern: Concentrating in Formerly Undervalued Black Neighborhoods**

The U.S. House Financial Services Subcommittee investigation found the five largest institutional investors purchased homes in neighborhoods where the percentage of Black residents was **over three times the national average** (40.2% average Black population in their top 20 ZIP codes, versus the national Black population of 13.4%). ([Brookings, "Single-family rentals: Trends and policy recommendations," November 2023](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/20231102_THP_SingleFamilyRentals_Proposal.pdf))

Research by Immergluck (2018) on Atlanta — the most institutionally saturated U.S. SFR market — found that increases in institutional SFR holdings were **concentrated in older, inner-county neighborhoods correlated with greater concentrations of Asian, Black, and Latino residents**. Atlanta has 72,000 mega investor-owned SFR units (27.17% of all SFR properties), making it the clearest national case study for Dallas researchers.

**The Segregation Business Model**

Per Shelterforce's analysis of the Congressional investigation data and the Redress Movement's 2022 policy brief:

> "Corporate landlords are purchasing homes in neighborhoods where the percentage of Black residents is over three times their level of representation in the U.S., where home prices are cheaper than in average neighborhoods, where average rents are conversely higher than in average neighborhoods, and where levels of homeownership are actually higher than in most neighborhoods across the country."

The business model exploits the racial wealth gap: properties in historically redlined Black neighborhoods are **artificially undervalued** due to systematic disinvestment, allowing institutional investors to purchase them at a discount. The same neighborhoods then generate **above-average rents relative to purchase price** because rental demand is high (Black households are shut out of homeownership by discriminatory lending). ([Shelterforce, "Corporate Landlords Profit from Segregation," October 2022](https://shelterforce.org/2022/10/19/corporate-landlords-profit-from-segregation-at-cost-of-black-homeownership-and-wealth/); [Redress Movement, "Corporate Landlords and Racial Redress Policy Brief," November 2022](https://redressmovement.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/Corporate-Landlords-and-Racial-Redress-Brief.pdf))

**Homeownership Effects**

An analysis of Atlanta (2007–2016) found that neighborhoods with higher institutional investor share saw a **larger drop in homeownership** — specifically, no difference in white homeownership rate change, but a **significant negative effect on Black homeownership**. ([Brookings, citing An (2023)](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/20231102_THP_SingleFamilyRentals_Proposal.pdf))

**Invitation Homes Headquarters Note**

Invitation Homes, Inc. — the nation's largest SFR REIT with approximately 86,139 rental homes as of early 2026 — is headquartered in Dallas's Lincoln Center building. Its first home purchase was in April 2012, and within one year it had spent $4 billion on 24,000 homes. It has a joint venture with Rockpoint Group (announced October 2020) to purchase $1 billion in single-family homes in **Dallas**, Seattle, South Florida, and other markets. ([Invitation Homes Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invitation_Homes); [ResiClub, January 2026](https://www.resiclubanalytics.com/p/institutional-landlord-invitation-homes-homebuilding-housing-market-build-to-rent-bet-resibuilt))

**Dallas Rental Housing Needs**

The City of Dallas Rental Housing Needs Assessment (2024) found the city faces a **39,919-unit rental housing supply gap** for low-income residents earning at or below 50% AMI ($48,700 for a family of four in 2022). ([Dallas CPAL Rental Housing Needs Assessment, 2024](https://wild-craft.imgix.net/cms.cpal.wild.plus/CPAL-Rental-Housing-Needs-Assessment-2024.pdf))

## 5. Public-Private Partnership (PPP) Ownership Structures

### Dallas PPP Program Structure

The City of Dallas's Public/Private Partnership Program provides economic support through:
- **Tax Abatement (Chapter 312):** Temporary abatement of real property or business personal property taxes
- **Business Development Chapter 380 Grants:** Grants for economic development, job creation, investment
- **New Markets Tax Credits (NMTC):** 39% federal tax credit payable over seven years for development in low-income neighborhoods

**Differential eligibility thresholds** create an inherent geographic bias:
- Southern Dallas: Only **25 jobs or $1 million investment** to qualify for incentives
- Northern Dallas & CBD: **100 jobs or $5 million investment** minimum

While the lower bar for Southern Dallas may appear favorable, the result is that South Dallas receives smaller deals with proportionally lower absolute dollar investments, while Northern Dallas deals receive larger absolute subsidies. ([City of Dallas, Public/Private Partnership Program Review, April 2016](https://dallascityhall.com/government/Council%20Meeting%20Documents/eco_3_public-private-partnership-program-review_combined_040416.pdf); [City of Dallas PPP Program Guidelines, June 2022](https://dallasecodev.org/DocumentCenter/View/3522/Public-Private-Partnership-Program-Guidelines-Criteria-6-22-Update))

### AT&T Discovery District

**Partners:** AT&T Inc. (corporate capital), Gensler (global architecture/design firm), City of Dallas (public approvals and coordination), Dallas Area Rapid Transit (infrastructure), Downtown Dallas Inc. (BID-style management nonprofit)

AT&T invested approximately **$100 million** developing its Discovery District — a four-block mixed-use campus with outdoor media screens, a food hall, and public gathering space, opened in 2021. The district's design was billed as a public-private collaboration to catalyze downtown revival. In January 2026, AT&T announced it would move its global headquarters to Plano, leaving the future of the Discovery District uncertain. ([AT&T Commits $100M to Redevelopment, D Magazine, January 2017](https://www.dmagazine.com/business-economy/2017/01/att-commits-100m-to-redevelopment-in-downtown-dallas/); [KERA, "Future of AT&T in Downtown Dallas Uncertain," January 2026](https://www.keranews.org/business-economy/2026-01-07/future-of-att-in-downtown-dallas-uncertain-after-plano-headquarter-announcement-discovery-district))

**Ownership of AT&T's primary towers:** In 2018, Woods Capital and Dundon Capital Partners acquired One AT&T Plaza (37-story Class A office tower) from Icahn Enterprises. Woods Capital is a Dallas-based real estate firm. The transaction was brokered by CBRE. ([Woods Capital press release, August 2018](https://www.woodscapital.com/single-post/2018/08/27/woods-capital-acquires-global-headquarters-for-att-in-downtown-dallas-with-dundon-capital))

**ULI award:** The AT&T Discovery District won a 2025 ULI award for "extensive five-year collaboration between AT&T, Gensler, and City of Dallas." No minority equity ownership is documented in the available public record. ([Urban Land Institute, AT&T Discovery District profile, 2025](https://uli.secure-platform.com/a/gallery/rounds/136/details/8655))

### Southern Dallas PPP and the Investment Asymmetry

From the 2016 PPP Program Review, as of that date, **44 of 57 PPP projects (78.6%) were in Southern Dallas** — but this represents smaller deals: 44 Southern Dallas projects received a combined $66 million ($33.25M tax abatements + $32.6M cash grants), compared to 7 Northern Dallas projects and 6 downtown projects. The disparity reflects that Southern Dallas projects receive smaller absolute subsidies due to lower baseline investment levels. ([City of Dallas PPP Program Review, 2016](https://dallascityhall.com/government/Council%20Meeting%20Documents/eco_3_public-private-partnership-program-review_combined_040416.pdf))

The City's 2021 Economic Development Policy acknowledges the fundamental gap:

> "Impacts of discriminatory policies, redlining and disinvestment have stifled prosperity and vitality in Southern Dallas. Communities there have a lower median household income, some of the highest rates of infection of COVID-19 and worse health outcomes."

The policy explicitly calls for "Prioritizing Southern Dallas and communities of color for whom structural disparities have hindered opportunity" and designates Southern Dallas as the geographic focus of the 10-year development agenda. ([City of Dallas Economic Development Policy 2022–2032](https://content.govdelivery.com/attachments/TXDALLAS/2021/05/26/file_attachments/1834960/CITY%20OF%20DALLAS%20ECONOMIC%20DEVELOPMENT%20POLICY.pdf))

### PPP Structures and Racial Exclusion: National Research

The national record on urban renewal and PPP structures documents a systematic pattern:

- **Urban renewal (1949–1974):** The Housing Act of 1949 financed public-private redevelopment in which **63% of the people relocated were African American**. In 2,500 housing projects in 1,000 cities, an estimated 4 million people were displaced, approximately 3 million of them Black. "Urban renewal" was known in Black communities as "Negro removal." ([National Academies Press, "Urban Renewal and the Production of Inequalities," 2016](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK395651/))
- **Ongoing legacy:** A 2025 law review article at Rutgers argues that urban renewal constituted "race-conscious government policymaking" — a 25-year federal-local program designed to promote white economic interests while purging and segregating Black urban residents. The program "institutionalized cumulative racial harms that have stretched beyond the reach of legal remedies." ([Rutgers Law/racism.org, "Urban Renewal's Grandchildren," June 2025](https://racism.org/articles/basic-needs/propertyland/12323-urban-renewals-grandchildren))
- **Dallas-specific ICP case:** ICP v. TDHCA (Texas Dept. of Housing) exposed that year after year, the state agency funded developers to build subsidized apartments almost exclusively in low-income, predominantly African American neighborhoods in South Dallas — and rarely in less segregated, higher income, majority white neighborhoods. HUD's 2013 investigation found "a pattern of negative reactions to projects that would provide affordable housing in the Northern Sector of Dallas." ([Shelterforce, "No Going Back to Segregation After Landmark Texas Fair Housing Case," September 2016](https://shelterforce.org/2016/09/26/no-going-back-to-segregation-after-landmark-texas-fair-housing-case/))
- **COVID-19 PPP exclusion:** The Paycheck Protection Program distributed during COVID-19 was slow to reach Black-owned businesses. The first round of PPP loans gave relief only to employer firms, which "disproportionately disregarded Black-owned businesses, 95% of which are sole proprietorships." ([Brookings, "Black-owned businesses in U.S. cities," February 2022](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/black-owned-businesses-in-u-s-cities-the-challenges-solutions-and-opportunities-for-prosperity/))

### Dallas Cedars Affordable Housing PPP (2026 Completion)

A notable exception: The Marcus, a 76-unit affordable housing development in the Cedars neighborhood (historically redlined), was completed in early 2026 through a public-private partnership including a **$15 million equity investment** from the AIC CEI-Boulos Opportunity Fund (joint venture of Allivate Impact Capital, a subsidiary of Woodforest Financial Group, and CEI-Boulos Capital Management). The City of Dallas participated, and the development uses a Public Facility Corporation (PFC) structure guaranteeing 60-year affordability. Notably, this project combined OZ equity with other subsidies. Developer: Savoy Equity Partners. ([Allivate Impact Capital, "The Marcus," press release](https://allivate.com/newsroom/))

## 6. Vendor/Contractor Diversity in Public Contracts

### National Research on Racial Disparities in Municipal Procurement

State and local governments spend approximately **$1.3 trillion annually** through public procurement. Historical evidence across hundreds of disparity studies documents systematic underutilization of MBE/WBE firms. ([Urban Institute, "Removing Barriers to Participation," May 2024](https://www.urban.org/research/publication/removing-barriers-participation-local-and-state-government-procurement))

**Key National Data Points:**

- **New York City (FY 2022):** Black-owned vendors received **under 2%** of the value of City contracts. Latino-owned firms received under 2%. In total, just **5.2% of all new City contracts** went to certified M/WBEs. White women or Asian American men received 70% of the total value of M/WBE contracts. Average contract value for M/WBE firms: $670,000 — **eight times smaller** than the $5.01 million average for non-M/WBE firms. ([NYC Comptroller, "Share of City's Contracting with M/WBEs Remains Woefully Small," February 2023](https://comptroller.nyc.gov/newsroom/share-of-citys-contracting-with-minority-women-owned-businesses-remains-woefully-small-comptroller-report-finds/))
- **San Diego (FY 2022):** 314 construction contracts were granted to Caucasian companies; **only four were awarded to African American businesses**. For architecture and engineering consultants: 308 Caucasian firms received contracts, **zero to identified African American firms**. A disparity study found African-American, Native-American, and woman-owned contractors received 20 to 36 cents on every dollar they should have received based on market availability. ([San Diego Voice & Viewpoint, October 2023](https://sdvoice.info/citys-contract-awards-fail-minority-businesses/))
- **Cuyahoga County, Ohio (2014–2018):** From $1.1 billion in contracts, only **0.59% went to minority-owned businesses**, despite 21% of available construction firms in the area being minority-owned. A follow-up 2020 study reached similar conclusions to a 2014 study. ([Journalist's Resource, "Disparity studies on municipal contracting," February 2021](https://journalistsresource.org/economics/disparity-studies-municipal-contracting/))
- **Harris County, TX (2016–2019):** M/WBEs received only **9.1% of $1.26 billion** awarded to prime contractors, compared to their **28.4% availability** — a disparity ratio of 32 cents on the dollar (vs. the 80-cents threshold for legal discrimination claims). Harris Health System awarded **0% of its contracts to Black-owned businesses** from 2018 through 2019. ([Community Impact / Colette Holt & Associates study, July 2022](https://communityimpact.com/houston/bay-area/city-county/2022/07/11/minority-women-owned-businesses-awarded-greater-share-of-harris-county-contracts/))
- **Boston (2014–2019):** While minority-owned firms were available for **11.3%** of Boston's subcontracting dollars, only **5.5% of subcontracting dollars** went to these firms. ([Urban Institute, 2024](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2024-04/Removing%20Barriers%20to%20Participation%20in%20Local%20and%20State%20Government%20Procurement%20and%20Contracting%20for%20Entrepreneurs%20of%20Color.pdf))
- **Hamilton County, Ohio (2016–2020):** Minority-owned firms were available for close to **16% of county contracting dollars** but received **less than 4%**. ([Urban Institute, 2024](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2024-04/Removing%20Barriers%20to%20Participation%20in%20Local%20and%20State%20Government%20Procurement%20and%20Contracting%20for%20Entrepreneurs%20of%20Color.pdf))
- **Federal (FY 2020):** Almost **40% of government small-business procurement dollars** went to contractors of color — a higher rate than most cities, reflecting decades of SBA 8(a) and DBE requirements. ([Urban Institute, 2024](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2024-04/Removing%20Barriers%20to%20Participation%20in%20Local%20and%20State%20Government%20Procurement%20and%20Contracting%20for%20Entrepreneurs%20of%20Color.pdf))

### Dallas-Specific Contracting Data

**Business Inclusion and Development (BID) Policy — Suspended in 2025**

Since 2008, the City of Dallas operated the Business Inclusion and Development (BID) Policy requiring M/WBE participation goals on contracts over $50,000. As of October 2020, goals were:

| Contract Type | M/WBE Goal |
|---|---|
| Architecture & Engineering | 34% |
| Construction | 32% |
| Professional Services | 38% |
| Goods | 32% |

In June 2025, **the BID Policy was suspended** in response to President Trump's January 2025 Executive Order 14173 requiring race and sex neutrality in government contracts. The City is now developing a replacement framework, the "Developing Regional & Inclusive Vendor Enterprises Framework (DRIVE)," intended to focus on small local businesses rather than race-specific criteria. ([Dallas Free Press, "Dallas policy for minority-owned businesses shifts," November 2025](https://dallasfreepress.com/dallas-news/dallas-policy-for-minority-owned-businesses-shifts-after-trumps-anti-dei-executive-order-focus-remains-on-equity/); [City of Dallas, Exhibit A BID Policy, 2020](https://dallascityhall.com/departments/procurement/DCH%20Documents/Exhibit-A-Business-Inclusion-Policy-2020.pdf))

**Actual BID Policy Performance (2023 Sample)**

From an October 2023 City Council agenda BID matrix, reviewing 4 items with M/WBE goals:
- 1 item **exceeded** the goal
- 1 item **met** the goal
- 2 items **did not meet** the goal (but complied with the policy's good-faith effort requirements)

This sample suggests that even under the BID Policy, actual utilization frequently fell below goals. ([City of Dallas Memorandum, October 2023](https://dallas.gov/government/citymanager/Documents/FY23-24%20Memos/Council%20Memos_102023.pdf))

**Dallas Good Jobs First Subsidy Tracker**

Texas's top subsidy recipients are overwhelmingly large corporations: Texas Instruments ($4.07 billion in cumulative state subsidies), Samsung ($1.86 billion), ExxonMobil ($1.36 billion). No Black-owned or minority-owned firms appear in the top 100 Texas subsidy recipients tracked by Good Jobs First. ([Good Jobs First Subsidy Tracker, Texas](https://subsidytracker.goodjobsfirst.org/state/TX))

### Structural Barriers to MBE Participation (Urban Institute, 2024)

Key structural barriers identified across multiple jurisdictions:
1. Cumbersome, fast-turnaround solicitation processes that disadvantage small firms
2. Onerous certification processes across multiple programs (DBE, SBE, WBE, LBE, MBE) with inconsistencies by jurisdiction
3. Contracts designed for large firms (high bonding/insurance requirements)
4. Legacy tracking systems that cannot monitor subcontractor spending
5. Delayed payments creating cash flow crises for small MBEs
6. Largest firms repeatedly winning entrenches patterns of exclusion
7. 600+ disparity studies commissioned at ~$1 million each — primarily to provide legal justification for race-conscious programs, rather than to remedy discrimination directly

## 7. Cross-Cutting Synthesis

### The Compounding Capital Stack

The research reveals that the capital instruments shaping neighborhood investment and displacement do not operate in isolation — they function as a compounding stack:

1. **TIF districts** in Dallas capture property tax increment in already-appreciating areas (Downtown Connection: $8.83 billion increment; Grand Park South: $333 million), diverting school and county revenues to subsidize development that largely benefits wealthier neighborhoods. The affordable housing produced (40% north of I-30 per city's own data) is structurally insufficient.

2. **Opportunity Zones** were designated in distressed tracts but capital systematically flows to already-appreciating zones within the OZ universe — 75% of investment to the top 20% by commercial investment density. For Dallas specifically, Texas does not make OZ transaction data public, preventing local analysis.

3. **PIDs** function as a legal mechanism to privatize enhanced municipal services in wealthy neighborhoods. Uptown ($4.17 million/year in supplemental revenue) and the Dallas Downtown Improvement District ($13.5 million/year) dwarf South Side ($411,000) and RedBird ($320,000), concentrating service quality improvements in already-advantaged areas.

4. **Institutional investors** in Dallas hold 26,961+ SFR units with a documented slight overconcentration in Black-majority census tracts relative to the MSA average, suppressing homeownership rates in neighborhoods already targeted by redlining. The pattern nationally and in Atlanta (the closest comparable market) shows corporate landlords using racial segregation as a profit engine.

5. **PPP structures** in Dallas have formally committed to Southern Dallas investment but the structural gap — Southern Dallas holds 15% of tax base for 45% of population, with 2.8x less small-business capital per capita than Dallas County overall — means PPPs in the South are proportionally much smaller in absolute dollar terms than those in North Dallas and Downtown.

6. **MBE contracting** historically reached 32–38% goals under Dallas's BID Policy, but the policy was suspended in 2025. Nationally, Black-owned firms consistently receive 20–36 cents on the dollar they should receive based on market availability, a pattern documented from Cuyahoga to San Diego to New York.

### The NCRC 50-Year Record

NCRC's 2025 "Displaced by Design" report documents the long-run outcome of these capital stack patterns: **523 majority-Black neighborhoods experienced gentrification and displacement between 1980 and 2020**, with 155 experiencing full racial turnover. An estimated 261,000–500,000 fewer Black people now live in formerly Black gentrifying neighborhoods. Dallas is specifically named as one of the cities where significant Black-to-Hispanic demographic transition occurred — a pattern driven by overlapping capital and policy mechanisms. ([NCRC, "Displaced by Design," May 2025](https://ncrc.org/displaced-by-design/))
