## 1. Brookings Institution: Investment Gaps, Capital Deserts, and Devaluation

### 1.1 The Foundational Devaluation Finding (2018)

The 2018 Brookings Metropolitan Policy Program study by Andre Perry, Jonathan Rothwell, and David Harshbarger remains one of the most-cited quantitative analyses of racial investment gaps in Black neighborhoods:

- **Owner-occupied homes in Black neighborhoods are undervalued by $48,000 per home on average**, amounting to **$156 billion in cumulative losses** nationwide.
- Homes in neighborhoods where the share of the population is 50% Black are valued at **roughly half the price** as homes in neighborhoods with no Black residents.
- With a full set of neighborhood and housing controls, Black homes are devalued by **19%–22%**, depending on methodology.
- The devaluation effect holds in **117 of 119** metropolitan areas with majority-Black neighborhoods.

> Source: [Perry, Rothwell & Harshbarger (2018), "The Devaluation of Assets in Black Neighborhoods," Brookings Institution](https://www.brookings.edu/wp-content/uploads/2018/11/2018.11_Brookings-Metro_Devaluation-Assets-Black-Neighborhoods_final.pdf)

### 1.2 Commercial Real Estate Devaluation (2022)

A 2022 Brookings study extended the devaluation analysis to commercial real estate (CRE):

- In majority-Black ZIP codes, devaluation results in aggregate wealth losses of:
  - **$235 billion** for residential real estate
  - **$171 billion** for retail (commercial) real estate
- Retail space is **undervalued by 7%** in majority-Black ZIP codes, even after controlling for economic predictors.
- Office space devaluation (1%) is not statistically significant — the retail-vs-office divergence suggests **consumer behavior and market segregation** (rather than purely structural factors) drive retail devaluation.

**Commercial real estate ownership disparities:**
- Only **3% of Black households** own nonresidential commercial real estate, versus **8% of white households**.
- Average white CRE-owning household holds **$34,000** in commercial real estate; average Black CRE-owning household holds **$3,600** — a **9.4:1 ratio**.
- The top 1% of CRE owners control **81% of all CRE value** nationally; top 1% of nonprimary residential property owners control **49%** of that category.

> Source: [Perry et al. (2022), "The Devaluation of Assets in Black Neighborhoods: The Case of Commercial Property," Brookings Institution](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-devaluation-of-assets-in-black-neighborhoods-the-case-of-commercial-property/)

### 1.3 Appraisal Bias and the Racial Housing Market (2021–2022)

The Brookings Institution conducted major research on how racial bias in appraisals propagates devaluation:

- Homes in majority-Black neighborhoods are valued **roughly 21%–23% below** what they would be in non-Black neighborhoods — a finding robust across multiple data years.
- Appraisal bias (where appraisers systematically undervalue homes relative to contract price) explains approximately **9%–19% of overall devaluation**, depending on modeling.
- Appraisal transactions in majority-Black neighborhoods are **1.9 times more likely to be appraised under the contract price** than homes in majority-white neighborhoods.
- An additional **6%** of homes in Black neighborhoods are under-appraised relative to the contract price (13% vs. 7%).
- The **median appraisal is 15% lower** in majority-Black census tracts compared to neighborhoods with less than 1% Black population, even controlling for sale prices and home/neighborhood characteristics.
- Even in the absence of appraisal bias, homes in Black neighborhoods would still be undervalued by approximately **20%** — meaning **lending, underwriting, and consumer behavior** drive the bulk of devaluation.

**Freddie Mac corroboration (12 million appraisals, 2015–2020):**
- **12.5%** of homes in mostly Black census tracts appraised below the sale price, vs. just **7.4%** in predominantly white areas.
- In predominantly Latino neighborhoods: **15.4%** appraised below sale price.
- The appraisal gap increases as the concentration of Black or Latino residents in census tracts increases.

> Sources:  
> [Rothwell & Perry (2022), "How Racial Bias in Appraisals Affects the Devaluation of Homes in Majority-Black Neighborhoods," Brookings Institution](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/how-racial-bias-in-appraisals-affects-the-devaluation-of-homes-in-majority-black-neighborhoods/)  
> [Brookings (2021), "Biased Appraisals and the Devaluation of Housing in Black Neighborhoods"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/biased-appraisals-and-the-devaluation-of-housing-in-black-neighborhoods/)  
> [Bankrate (2024), "The Black Neighborhood Home Appraisal Gap"](https://www.bankrate.com/mortgages/homes-in-black-neighborhoods-more-likely-to-get-low-appraisals/)

### 1.4 The Three "Ds": Devaluation, Disinvestment, Displacement

Brookings research articulates a structural pattern in Black commercial corridors and neighborhoods:

1. **Devaluation** — Systematic undervaluation of existing assets (residential and commercial property).
2. **Disinvestment** — As values drop, banks and investors withdraw, creating reinforcing cycles that inhibit business creation and scaling.
3. **Displacement** — Once asset devaluation depresses prices below a threshold, outside (typically white) developers buy cheaply and leverage ownership into development schemes that displace long-term residents.

> Source: [Brookings (2021), "'40 Acres and a Mall': How Community Ownership Models Can Preserve Economic Power in Black Neighborhoods"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/40-acres-and-a-mall-how-community-ownership-models-can-preserve-economic-power-in-black-neighborhoods/)

### 1.5 Business Capital Gaps (2022–2025)

Brookings research quantifies barriers to capital formation for Black entrepreneurs:

- Black Americans represent **14.4%** of the U.S. population but only **3.3%–3.4% of employer businesses**.
- Homes in majority-Black neighborhoods are systematically undervalued by a cumulative total of **$162 billion**, limiting the collateral base for business loans.
- Young adults (25–29) in Black-majority neighborhoods have a median credit score of **582**, compared to **644** in majority-Latino neighborhoods and **687** in majority-white neighborhoods.
- Even among firms with relatively high credit scores, **Black-owned businesses were half as likely** as white-owned counterparts to receive all the financing they sought.
- Black women business owners face a loan rejection rate **triple** that of white business owners; **61%** self-fund.
- In 2023, **venture capital investments in Black entrepreneurs dropped 71%**, with less than **1%** of those dollars going to Black startups.

> Sources:  
> [Brookings (2025), "Reaping the Unrealized Gains of Black Businesses"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/reaping-the-unrealized-gains-of-black-businesses/)  
> [Brookings (2026), "Black Employers Are Reaching New Heights"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/black-employers-are-reaching-new-heights/)

### 1.6 Racial Wealth Gap Context

- In 2022, for every **$100 in wealth** held by white households, Black households held only **$15**.
- Median Black household wealth: **$44,890** (2022) vs. median white household wealth — a gap of **$240,120**.
- Stock equity (driven by capital gains and investment access) made up nearly **30% of white wealth** growth but only **4% of Black wealth** growth from 2019–2022 — reflecting the structural exclusion of Black households from investment capital markets.

> Source: [Brookings (2024), "Black Wealth Is Increasing, But So Is the Racial Wealth Gap"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/black-wealth-is-increasing-but-so-is-the-racial-wealth-gap/)

## 2. Urban Institute: Racial Disparities in Capital Flows and CDFI Activity

### 2.1 City-Level Investment Disparities

The Urban Institute has produced the most detailed spatial analyses of private capital flows by neighborhood racial composition:

**Chicago (Theodos et al. 2019):**
- The typical majority-white neighborhood received **over 4.5 times** the level of private investment per household as the typical majority-Black neighborhood.

**Baltimore (Theodos et al. 2019–2020, covering 2004–2016):**
- Neighborhoods less than 50% Black received **nearly 4 times** the per household investment as neighborhoods more than 85% Black.
- Specifically: **$26,533** per household per year in neighborhoods under 50% Black vs. **$8,160** per household per year in neighborhoods over 85% Black.
- White neighborhoods received **more than 3 times** the total investment of majority-Black neighborhoods over the 13-year period.
- Public funding and mission lending were more evenly distributed but represented only a **fraction of total investment** — insufficient to offset private capital gaps.

> Sources:  
> [Urban Institute (2020), "A New Agenda for Community Development Finance"](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/103161/a-new-agenda-for-community-development-finance.pdf)  
> [Urban Institute (2020), "Disinvestment in Baltimore's Black Neighborhoods Is Foreboding — Reversible"](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/disinvestment-baltimores-black-neighborhoods-foreboding-reversible)

**Cleveland (Theodos et al. 2023):**
- Majority-white neighborhoods received **over two times** the average aggregate capital flows per household of majority-Black neighborhoods.
- Catalytic (mission) capital would need to be **20 times greater** to make up for deficits in mainstream (private) capital provision.

> Source: [Urban Institute (2024), "Catalytic Capital for Neighborhood Reinvestment"](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2024-03/Catalytic_Capital_for_Neighborhood_Reinvestment.pdf)

**PPP Loans (Chicago, 2020–2021):**
- During Phases 1 and 2 of the Paycheck Protection Program, only **4.6%** and **8.2%** of loan dollars went to majority-Black and Latinx neighborhoods respectively, while **71.7%** went to majority-white tracts.
- Majority-Black neighborhoods received **$49.9 billion in mortgage investments** in 2019 — just **2.3%** of all U.S. mortgage loan dollars.

> Source: [Lester & Wilson (2023), "The Racial and Spatial Impacts of the Paycheck Protection Program," University of Illinois at Chicago/UIC Great Cities Institute](https://greatcities.uic.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/942/2026/01/lester-wilson-2023-the-racial-and-spatial-impacts-of-the-paycheck-protection-program.pdf)

### 2.2 CDFI Data and Structural Limits

Urban Institute research reveals the structural limits of CDFIs as a substitute for mainstream private capital:

- Between **2011 and 2015**, **27%** of U.S. counties saw **no CDFI lending activity**.
- **50%** of all counties saw annual CDFI lending activity amounting to **less than $7** for every person earning below 200% of the federal poverty level.
- CDFI total assets: **$222 billion** (2019/2020) — representing **less than 1%** of the **$18.3 trillion** combined total assets of FDIC-insured banks and NCUA-insured credit unions (2017).
- More than **40% of CDFIs' loans and investments** go to majority-minority communities.
- More than **a third** of CDFIs are led by minorities.
- CDFIs approve applications at **twice the rate** of large banks.
- However, **white-led CDFIs hold approximately 6 times** the assets of minority-led CDFIs; CDFIs led by people of color hold roughly **10%** of all CDFI loan fund assets.

**CDFI investment flows (2019):**
- $25 billion in small business and microlending
- $56 billion toward housing
- $59 billion in consumer loans
- $24 billion toward commercial real estate
- $4 billion toward community facilities

> Sources:  
> [Urban Institute (2020), "A New Agenda for Community Development Finance"](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/103161/a-new-agenda-for-community-development-finance.pdf)  
> [Urban Institute (2021), "A New Era of Racial Equity in Community Development Finance"](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/105256/a-new-era-of-racial-equity-in-community-development-finance_0_0.pdf)  
> [Zenith Wealth Partners (2026), "Where Capital Goes Shapes Communities"](https://zenithwealth.partners/institutions/where-capital-goes-shapes-communities/)

### 2.3 Mortgage Denial Rates and Housing Wealth

- **More than 24%** of Black families who applied for home purchase loans were denied in 2022, compared with **11%** of white applicants — a rate **more than twice** as high.
- Black applicants are denied mortgages at a rate **84% higher** than that of white applicants (Bachaud 2022).
- The Black homeownership rate was **44.3%** in 2022 vs. **65.1%** for all households — a 30-percentage-point gap wider than it was when segregation was legal in 1960.
- Black households held **5.9%** of total housing wealth in 2022 despite making up **11.9%** of households.
- Total value of homes owned by Black households: **$2.17 trillion** in 2022 (up from $1.11 trillion in 2015).

> Sources:  
> [Urban Institute (2024), "Black Homeownership Increased Slightly during the Pandemic, but High Interest Rates Threaten These Gains"](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/black-homeownership-increased-slightly-during-pandemic-high-interest-rates-threaten)  
> [Urban Institute (2025), "Black Housing Wealth Varies across Local Markets"](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/black-housing-wealth-varies-across-local-markets-despite-recent-improvement-black)

### 2.4 Systemic Racism in Mortgage Underwriting

Urban Institute research establishes that standard underwriting criteria themselves encode racial exclusion:

- The median credit score for Black people is **629**, nearly **100 points lower** than the median score for white people.
- According to 2020 HMDA data, median debt-to-income ratio for Black mortgage applicants: **40.0%** vs. **33.0%** for white applicants.
- Median loan-to-value ratio for Black applicants: **69.7%** vs. **64.5%** for white applicants.
- The "three Cs" of mortgage underwriting (credit, collateral, capacity) are all outcomes of historical racial discrimination — meaning ostensibly race-neutral underwriting standards perpetuate discriminatory outcomes.

> Source: [Urban Institute (2021), "Closing the Homeownership Gap Will Require Rooting Systemic Racism Out of Mortgage Underwriting"](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/closing-homeownership-gap-will-require-rooting-systemic-racism-out-mortgage-underwriting)

### 2.5 Racial Equity Commitments Post-2020

Between June 2020 and May 2021:
- Private and philanthropic sectors made at least **$215 billion** in racial equity commitments.
- More than **$200 billion** came from the private sector (primarily banks and financial institutions: **$40+ billion**).
- **$12.6 billion** in philanthropic grants toward racial equity in 2020; **$11.6 billion** in additional philanthropic commitments.
- Commitments were largely planned for full distribution between **2023 and 2025**.
- Despite this, structural investment gaps persist because commitments were often one-time, not systemic.

> Source: [Urban Institute (2021), "A New Era of Racial Equity in Community Development Finance"](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/105256/a-new-era-of-racial-equity-in-community-development-finance_0_0.pdf)

## 3. McKinsey Institute for Black Economic Mobility: The Economic Opportunity Gap

### 3.1 The $1.6 Trillion Business Revenue Gap (2021)

McKinsey's landmark 2021 report establishes the scale of the capital formation gap:

- In a **parity scenario**, Black-owned businesses would generate **$1.6 trillion more** than they do today:
  - If Black share of business ownership matched Black share of the population, **615,000 more enterprises** would exist, generating **~$1 trillion** in additional revenue.
  - If existing Black-owned firms matched the average scale of industry peers, they would add **another $600 billion** in revenue.
- **70%** of the aggregate revenue gap exists within just five industries: wholesale trade, retail trade, construction, manufacturing, and professional services.
- Only **1%** of venture capital funding goes to Black founders (Crunchbase, 2021 data).
- White entrepreneurs start with **$107,000** in startup capital on average; Black founders start with **$35,000** — a **3:1 ratio**.
- **96%** of Black-owned businesses are sole proprietorships (vs. 80% of non-Black-owned).
- Black-owned employer firms generate only **60%** of the revenue produced by non-Black counterparts ($1M vs. $2.4M annually on average).

> Source: [McKinsey & Company (2021), "The Economic State of Black America: What Is and What Could Be"](https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/diversity-and-inclusion/the-economic-state-of-black-america-what-is-and-what-could-be)

### 3.2 The $700 Billion Consumer Value Opportunity

- Black consumer spending totaled approximately **$835–$910 billion** in 2019 (~10% of national total, below the 13.4% population share).
- Projected to reach **$1.7 trillion** in nominal dollars by 2030.
- If income parity and access were achieved, there is an opportunity to unlock **~$700 billion in value** shared between companies and Black households.
- Counties with populations that are majority people of color have **roughly 30% fewer banks** than majority-white counties.

> Source: [McKinsey & Company (2021/2023), "The State of Black Consumers: An Opportunity for Growth and Equity"](https://www.mckinsey.com/institute-for-economic-mobility/our-insights/black-consumers-and-the-opportunity-for-growth-and-equity)

### 3.3 Three Obstacles to Private Capital Deployment (2023)

McKinsey's 2023 impact investing guide identifies structural barriers preventing the **$350 billion** committed by U.S. investors from reaching Black communities:

1. **A gap between committed capital and needed capital** — pledges far exceed deployable capital structured for community benefit.
2. **A small number of Black-founded, -owned, and -led funds** — limiting the investment intermediary pipeline.
3. **A lack of clearly identified impactful investable themes** — investors lack a framework for what investments advance Black economic mobility.

The guide identifies **eight high-potential pillars** for impact investment: affordable housing, pre-K–12 education, health equity, financial inclusion, credit/ecosystem development for SMEs, workforce training, the digital divide, and public infrastructure.

> Source: [McKinsey Institute for Black Economic Mobility (2023), "A Guide to Impact Investing in Black Economic Mobility"](https://www.mckinsey.com/institute-for-economic-mobility/our-insights/a-guide-to-impact-investing-in-black-economic-mobility)

### 3.4 The Housing Investment Gap (2025)

The McKinsey Institute's 2025 housing report is among the most comprehensive analyses of the connection between private capital flows, housing, and Black economic mobility:

- **8.2 million fewer housing units** than needed as of 2023; projected to grow to **9.6 million unit deficit** by 2035 without action.
- **Nearly 60%** of Black renters and **30%** of Black homeowners are moderately or severely **cost burdened** — well above national averages.
- Nearly **50%** of Black families live in urban areas where rising prices and constrained supply are most prevalent.
- Closing the housing shortfall would require an investment of **$2.7 trillion** (cumulative) but could generate **nearly $2 trillion in GDP** and create **nearly 2 million jobs** by 2035.
- **Five housing themes** with highest potential: unlocking land through creative incentives and partnerships; augmenting programs to unleash private capital; scaling off-site home construction; reinvesting in public housing and shared-equity models; revamping housing choice vouchers.
- Together, these strategies could unlock an estimated **2.3 million housing units**, including **700,000 for Black households**, over the next decade.

> Source: [McKinsey Institute for Black Economic Mobility (2025), "Investing in Housing: Unlocking Economic Mobility for Black Families and All Americans"](https://www.mckinsey.com/institute-for-economic-mobility/our-insights/investing-in-housing-unlocking-economic-mobility-for-black-families-and-all-americans)

### 3.5 Geographic Disparities in Black Outcomes (2024)

McKinsey's 2024 place-based analysis finds:

- At the current pace of progress, it would take **more than three centuries** for Black residents across the nation to reach parity with their white neighbors.
- There is **nowhere in the United States** where outcomes for Black residents equal those of their white neighbors.
- Outcomes for Black residents are generally better in suburban and high-growth areas, but these have the **smallest Black populations**.
- Racial gaps in outcomes are **largest in megacities and stable cities**, which are home to ~50% of all Black Americans.

> Source: [McKinsey & Company (2024), "The Relevance of Place to Racial Equity and Outcomes"](https://www.mckinsey.com/institute-for-economic-mobility/our-insights/the-state-of-black-residents-the-relevance-of-place-to-racial-equity-and-outcomes)

## 4. Institutional Investors: REITs, Private Equity, and Single-Family Rental Displacement

### 4.1 Institutional Single-Family Rental Investors and Black Homeownership

Research from the Georgia Institute of Technology (2023) provides the most direct quantitative evidence of how institutional investors systematically crowd out Black homeownership:

- In the Atlanta metro area (2007–2016), Black people lost more than **$4 billion in home equity** due to institutional investor purchases — described as a "conservative, lower estimate."
- Investors acquired up to **76%** of for-sale single-family homes in some neighborhoods at peak concentration (2013).
- On average, neighborhoods experienced an increase in large investor purchases from nearly **0% in 2007** to over **12% in 2013** (peak year).
- The negative effect of institutional investors on homeownership is **much worse for Black homeownership and totally absent for white homeownership**.

> Source: [Georgia Institute of Technology / Brian An (2023), "The Influence of Institutional Single-Family Rental Investors on Homeownership," Journal of Planning Education and Research](https://news.gatech.edu/news/2023/08/07/investors-force-black-families-out-home-ownership-new-research-shows)

### 4.2 Corporate Landlords, Racial Segregation, and Displacement

Congressional subcommittee data and investigative reporting reveals the racial geography of institutional investment:

- Corporate landlords are purchasing homes in neighborhoods where **the percentage of Black residents is over 3 times** their level of representation in the U.S.
- In these neighborhoods: **home prices are cheaper than average**, average **rents are higher than average**, and **homeownership rates are actually higher** than in most neighborhoods.
- In Charlotte, NC: investors now own roughly **25,000 homes** in the Charlotte-Mecklenburg area — **1 of every 20 single-family homes** in the county. **25%** of total home purchases in 2021 were by investors.
- In Milwaukee: out-of-state landlords own **14% of all rental homes**; in predominantly Black north/west side neighborhoods, **3 of every 4 homes** are owned by corporate landlords.
- By 2021, at least five companies owned over **30,000 single-family homes** each; American Homes 4 Rent, Invitation Homes, and Progress Residential each owned over **50,000**.

> Sources:  
> [Shelterforce (2022), "Corporate Landlords Profit from Segregation"](https://shelterforce.org/2022/10/19/corporate-landlords-profit-from-segregation-at-cost-of-black-homeownership-and-wealth/)  
> [NACA (2022), "New Report Says Institutionalized Bias Spurs Racial Disparity in Black Homeownership"](https://www.naca.com/new-report-says-institutionalized-bias-spurs-racial-disparity-in-black-homeownership/)

### 4.3 Investor Purchases and Racial Transition (Atlanta)

Research in *Housing Policy Debate* (2021) on Atlanta (2010–2016):

- Investor purchases of apartments are associated with a **33% increase in the odds of an eviction spike** in the same year.
- Investor multifamily purchases resulted in **166 fewer Black residents and 109 additional white residents** per neighborhood compared to adjacent neighborhoods without an investor purchase.
- Average neighborhood population in the study: 1,635.
- Non-investor purchases are **not associated** with any increase in the odds of eviction spikes.

> Source: [NLIHC (2021), "Investor Purchases of Rental Housing Increase Eviction and Racial Transition in Neighborhoods"](https://nlihc.org/resource/investor-purchases-rental-housing-increase-eviction-and-racial-transition-neighborhoods)

### 4.4 Investors in Majority-Black Neighborhoods: 2021 Data

- In 2021, **30% of home sales** in majority-Black neighborhoods went to investors, compared with **12%** in other ZIP codes.

> Source: [Washington Post/Facebook analysis cited in Earn Your Leisure, 2024](https://www.facebook.com/earnyourleisure/posts/a-2022-study-by-ariel-investments-and-charles-schwab-highlights-a-significant-tr/771592285001861/)

### 4.5 Fintech vs. Small Banks in PPP Lending (NBER, 2021)

The NBER study of 6 million PPP loans reveals how human bias in lending concentrates capital away from Black-owned businesses:

- Black-owned businesses accounted for **3.3%** of loans issued by small banks but **26.5%** of loans made by fintech lenders.
- Fintech lenders made only **17.4%** of overall loans but processed **53.6%** of loans received by Black-owned businesses.
- In regions with higher racial animus, Black-owned businesses were **particularly likely** to get their PPP loans from fintech lenders and less likely to get them from small/mid-sized banks.
- **Share of PPP loans by small banks directed to Black-owned businesses rose substantially after automation** — confirming that human subjectivity in lending is a proximate cause of racial disparities.

> Source: [NBER (2021), "Racial Disparities in Access to Small Business Credit: Evidence from the Paycheck Protection Program," Working Paper 29364](https://www.nber.org/digest/202112/racial-disparities-paycheck-protection-program-lending)

## 5. Public-Private Partnerships: Who Benefits?

### 5.1 Opportunity Zones: Private Capital in Name Only for Black Communities

The Opportunity Zone (OZ) program — created by the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act to direct private capital gains investment to low-income census tracts — provides a case study of how PPP structures can bypass Black community needs:

- By spring 2020, **Opportunity Zone equity investments exceeded $10 billion**; by end of 2022, **$89 billion** in qualifying equity investments had been made across over 5,600 designated low-income neighborhoods.
- **Analysis by the Office of Tax Analysis** found that zones that received investment had:
  - Higher median home values ($242,000 vs. $136,000 for non-investment zones)
  - Higher median incomes ($43,000 vs. $36,000)
  - **Lower share of Black residents** than zones that did not receive investment
- Just under **a third of Opportunity Zones** are located in tracts with the lowest investment levels; **28%** are in tracts already attracting high levels of investment — minimal targeting toward the most disinvested areas.
- The average investment score for all designated OZs: **5.3** (out of 10); the average for non-designated areas: **5.5** — meaning OZs were not meaningfully more disinvested than non-designated tracts.
- An estimated **19% of OZ tracts** in 29 major cities are already undergoing gentrification — creating displacement pressure for the Black and Latino families most concentrated in these areas.
- High-profile early OZ projects featured **luxury apartment buildings and hotels** — projects that benefit investors using the tax subsidy for projects that would have occurred anyway.

> Sources:  
> [Tax Policy Center, "What Are Opportunity Zones and How Do They Work?"](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/what-are-opportunity-zones-and-how-do-they-work)  
> [Rockefeller Foundation (2021), "Making Opportunity Zones Work for Communities"](https://www.rockefellerfoundation.org/wp-content/uploads/2021/04/OZ-Report.pdf)  
> [Institute on Taxation and Economic Policy (2019), "Opportunity Zones Bolster Investors' Bottom Lines Rather than Economic or Racial Equity"](https://itep.org/opportunity-zones-bolster-investors-bottom-lines-rather-than-economic-or-racial-equity/)

### 5.2 Paycheck Protection Program as a Public-Private Partnership Failure

The PPP — administered through private lenders with federal guarantees — reproduced and amplified existing racial capital inequalities:

- During Phases 1 and 2 (through September 2020), only **4.6% and 8.2% of loan dollars** went to majority-Black and Latinx neighborhoods respectively.
- **71.7%** of PPP loan dollars went to majority-white tracts.
- Reliance on existing bank-client relationships and **geographic concentration of bank branches in white neighborhoods** structurally excluded Black-owned businesses.
- Black-owned businesses received **smaller and fewer PPP loans** than white-owned counterparts from the same lenders, even after controlling for firm characteristics.
- Policy design changes in Phase 3 (Biden administration reforms, February 2021) resulted in a meaningful shift: PPP loans per business in majority-Black neighborhoods increased by **79,000 loans (32%)**, demonstrating that structure, not demand, drove Phase 1-2 exclusion.

> Sources:  
> [UIC (2023), "The Racial and Spatial Impacts of the Paycheck Protection Program"](https://greatcities.uic.edu/wp-content/uploads/sites/942/2026/01/lester-wilson-2023-the-racial-and-spatial-impacts-of-the-paycheck-protection-program.pdf)

## 6. "Investability" as a Racial Construction

### 6.1 The Three Cs of Underwriting as Encoded Racial Exclusion

Research by the Urban Institute, Brookings, and the CFPB establishes that standard mortgage and business underwriting criteria are not race-neutral; they encode the cumulative outcomes of racial discrimination into ostensibly objective metrics:

**Credit Scores:**
- Credit scoring models **favor homeownership over renting** because mortgages establish debt repayment history. The racial homeownership gap (44% Black vs. 73% white) thus systematically produces lower credit scores for Black borrowers.
- Racial gaps in credit scores are **persistent over time and place**.
- **26% of Black-owned startups** are categorized as having high credit risk, compared to **16%** of startup owners overall.
- Median credit score in Black-majority neighborhoods for ages 25–29: **582** vs. **687** in majority-white neighborhoods — a 105-point gap that directly translates into differential loan terms, approval rates, and interest rates.
- Black credit scores are treated by lenders as if they were **71 points lower** than they actually are (one experimental study cited by Brookings).
- If credit scores for Black-owned businesses were treated in the same way as scores for white men, **Black businesses' credit lines would double**.

**Collateral:**
- Homes in majority-Black neighborhoods are undervalued by **$48,000** on average ($156 billion cumulatively), meaning Black homeowners have systematically less collateral available for business loans.
- The racially constructed undervaluation of collateral creates a compounding feedback loop: lower appraised values → less collateral → lower or denied loans → reduced business investment → further neighborhood disinvestment.

**Capacity (Debt-to-Income):**
- Black households carry disproportionately higher student loan debt (36% of Black graduates vs. 20% of white graduates in 2022) and other consumer debt — increasing DTI ratios and triggering loan denials.
- The median debt-to-income ratio for Black mortgage applicants (40.0%) exceeds the white applicant median (33.0%) — a gap directly attributable to racial wealth disparities, not individual behavior.

> Sources:  
> [Urban Institute (2021), "Closing the Homeownership Gap Will Require Rooting Systemic Racism Out of Mortgage Underwriting"](https://www.urban.org/urban-wire/closing-homeownership-gap-will-require-rooting-systemic-racism-out-mortgage-underwriting)  
> [Urban Institute (2024), "Evidence of Disparities in Access to Mortgage Credit"](https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/2024-03/Evidence_of_Disparities_in_Access_to_Mortgage_Credit.pdf)  
> [Brookings (2025), "Reaping the Unrealized Gains of Black Businesses"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/reaping-the-unrealized-gains-of-black-businesses/)

### 6.2 Appraisal Comparables: Structurally Racist Methodology

The appraisal process uses recently sold "comparable" homes (comps) to value a property. In racially segregated markets, this methodology:

- Locks in historical values produced by discriminatory policies (redlining, blockbusting, racial steering).
- **98% of appraisers are white** — limiting awareness of or accountability for neighborhood-level racial bias.
- Comparables selected in Black neighborhoods are themselves undervalued, creating a self-referential devaluation loop.
- Freddie Mac found that **a large portion of the professionals** who completed appraisals in both minority and non-minority areas generated statistically significant appraisal gaps — meaning the problem is systemic, not idiosyncratic.

> Sources:  
> [Bankrate (2024), "The Black Neighborhood Home Appraisal Gap"](https://www.bankrate.com/mortgages/homes-in-black-neighborhoods-more-likely-to-get-low-appraisals/)  
> [NACA (2022), "New Report Says Institutionalized Bias Spurs Racial Disparity"](https://www.naca.com/new-report-says-institutionalized-bias-spurs-racial-disparity-in-black-homeownership/)

### 6.3 Risk Ratings and "Market Information Gaps"

The Brookings Urban Markets Initiative identified **market information gaps** as a core mechanism by which Black neighborhoods appear "uninvestable":

- Investors and lenders rely on market signals (transaction volumes, property values, business revenues) that are themselves products of prior disinvestment.
- "Less investment begets less investment" — the absence of capital infrastructure (grocery stores, financial institutions, upgraded housing stock) creates the very conditions that investors cite as evidence of unviability.
- These information gaps are "a primary cause of devaluation" — not the underlying characteristics of homes, businesses, or residents.

The case of developer Brian Rice in Birmingham illustrates this dynamic: Rice sought a loan to renovate vacant buildings on the commercial corridor of the majority-Black Ensley neighborhood; a bank appraised the properties as "basically worthless" despite their clear development potential — rendering the neighborhood structurally "uninvestable" through the underwriting process.

> Source: [Brookings (2022), "The Devaluation of Assets in Black Neighborhoods: The Case of Commercial Property"](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/the-devaluation-of-assets-in-black-neighborhoods-the-case-of-commercial-property/)

### 6.4 Subprime Predatory Lending as Inverted Investability

The history of subprime lending in Black communities reveals that "disinvestment" and "predatory investment" are two sides of the same coin:

- When mainstream capital withdraws (disinvestment), predatory lenders enter with higher-cost products — targeting the exclusion created by conventional underwriting.
- From 2003–2009, Black homeowners who remained in their homes accrued **less home equity** than white homeowners and were **more likely to end the period with negative equity**.
- The real estate industry has historically charged African Americans **inflated prices for inferior properties**, knowing they had no alternative market access.
- Between 2007 and 2016, institutional investors built their portfolios primarily through **foreclosed homes** — a crisis that disproportionately impacted Black homeowners.

> Sources:  
> [Center for American Progress (2019), "Racial Disparities in Home Appreciation"](https://www.americanprogress.org/article/racial-disparities-home-appreciation/)  
> [Shelterforce (2022), "Corporate Landlords Profit from Segregation"](https://shelterforce.org/2022/10/19/corporate-landlords-profit-from-segregation-at-cost-of-black-homeownership-and-wealth/)

### 7.2 CDFI Ecosystem: Scale, Limits, and Racial Disparities Within

- **$222 billion** in total CDFI assets (2019/2020) — less than **1%** of the **$18.3 trillion** U.S. banking system.
- The estimated investment needed for fundamental change in an underserved community: **$500 million to $1 billion** over 10+ years.
- **27%** of U.S. counties had **no CDFI lending activity** between 2011–2015.
- Internal racial disparities within the CDFI ecosystem:
  - White-led CDFIs hold approximately **6 times** the assets of minority-led CDFIs.
  - **CDFI loan funds led by people of color** hold roughly **10%** of all loan fund assets.
  - Of CDFI Fund awards, minority-led institutions received only **26%**.
- **83%** of CDFI clients are low-income; **61%** are people of color.

## 8. Synthesis: The Architecture of Capital Exclusion

### 8.1 Compounding Mechanisms

Research across all six thematic areas reveals an interconnected system of capital exclusion rather than isolated phenomena:

```
HISTORICAL POLICY (Redlining, Segregation, FHA Exclusion)
    ↓
Lower Black homeownership rates (44% vs. 73% white)
    ↓
Lower collateral for loans + Lower intergenerational wealth
    ↓
Lower credit scores (582 median in Black ZIP vs. 687 in white ZIP)
    ↓
Higher loan denial rates (24% for Black vs. 11% for white, 2022)
    ↓
Less investment in Black neighborhoods
    ↓
Lower property values (appraisal gap: 15–23% below equivalent white neighborhood)
    ↓
Less collateral → more denials → reinforcing cycle
    ↓
"Uninvestable" status encoded in underwriting criteria
    ↓
Institutional investors enter: $4B equity extraction in Atlanta alone (2007–2016)
    ↓
Displacement, reduced Black homeownership, wealth extraction to external investors
```

### 8.2 Scale of the Investment Gap: Summary Statistics

| Metric | Data Point | Source |
|--------|------------|--------|
| Residential devaluation in majority-Black ZIP codes | $235 billion cumulative loss | Brookings (2022) |
| Commercial RE devaluation in majority-Black ZIP codes | $171 billion cumulative loss | Brookings (2022) |

### 8.3 Structural Recommendations Emerging from Research

Research across these institutions converges on several structural interventions:

1. **Reform underwriting criteria** to incorporate rent payment history, utility payments, and other non-debt repayment indicators of creditworthiness.
2. **Expand CDFI capacity** — the Urban Institute recommends expanding the CDFI Fund budget at least four-fold to $1 billion annually; CDFIs currently hold <1% of total banking system assets.
3. **Address appraisal bias systemically** — not through individual complaints, but through standardized accreditation, transparent metrics, and algorithmic value models.
4. **Design PPPs with explicit community benefit and anti-displacement requirements** — preventing the PPP and Opportunity Zone patterns of directing public subsidy to projects that benefit investors in Black neighborhoods rather than residents.
5. **Invest in Black-led financial intermediaries** — minority-led CDFIs and MDIs hold only 10–26% of CDFI assets; capital access for these institutions is the most direct path to community-aligned deployment.
6. **Close the VC gap through structural reform** — with only 0.4% of VC reaching Black founders in 2024, market incentives alone will not correct this imbalance.
