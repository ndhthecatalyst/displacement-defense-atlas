# Layer 3b: The Fiscal Extraction Sub-Layer
## Public Facility Corporations, Opportunity Zones, and CIP Procurement Geography

**Added:** April 25, 2026
**Associated hypotheses:** H7 (PFC), H8 (OZ), H9 (stacked inversion)
**Fellowship context:** BOHCDC Housing Justice Policy Fellowship application portfolio

---

## Overview

Layer 3b is a sub-layer of the Five-Layer Capital Stack framework, nested within Layer 3 (TIF/OZ financial engineering). It isolates three mechanisms that share a common operational logic: **public resources, structured to serve disinvested communities, are captured by well-capitalized actors concentrated in Dallas's more affluent northern geography, while the fiscal, social, and spatial costs of that capture are externalized onto South Dallas residents the programs were publicly justified to help.**

The three mechanisms are:
1. **Public Facility Corporations (PFCs)** — Chapter 303 Texas Local Government Code property tax exemptions for multifamily development
2. **Opportunity Zones (OZs)** — federal capital-gains tax shelter for investment in designated census tracts
3. **CIP Procurement Geography** — the vendor residue dimension of Capital Improvement Program contracting

Analyzed together, these three mechanisms constitute what this research terms a **stacked inversion**: the subsidy instruments are stacked on top of each other in Northern Dallas and gentrifying corridors (Cedars, West Dallas, Oak Cliff Gateway), inverted from their stated purpose of serving disinvested communities.

## Mechanism 1: Public Facility Corporations (H7)

### Instrument design
A PFC is a Texas nonprofit entity that allows a municipality, county, or housing authority to take title to a multifamily property, lease it back to a private developer under a long-term agreement, and grant a 100% property tax exemption for periods up to 60–75 years. Authorized by Chapter 303 of the Texas Local Government Code; the 2015 expansion allowed PFC-issued exemptions for apartment complexes.

### Fiscal erosion mechanism
- **LBB HB 2071 report (2024):** PFC exemptions increased the state's required Foundation School Program share by **$63.4 million** in the 2024–25 biennium — a cost shifted from school district ad valorem tax rolls upward to state general revenue
- **DISD exposure:** DISD tax rate $0.979735/$100 assessed value; DISD's 2026 proposed bond package = $6.2B (1-cent rate increase). The ad valorem base DISD relies on is being eroded by PFC exemptions on Northern Dallas multifamily developments
- **DPFC portfolio:** 35 properties carrying tax exemptions as of 2025; city reports $1,756,256 in forgone taxes — approximately $213/unit/year at city rate only; county + DISD + Parkland + Dallas College forgone taxes not included in city calculation
- **Affordability gap:** DPFC nominally requires 40% of units at or below 80% AMI; actual outcomes show significant slippage; 80% AMI band does not serve the income profile of South Dallas crisis-quadrant households

### Geographic concentration
- PFC-eligible developments cluster in areas with existing developer infrastructure, legal sophistication, and capital access — predominantly Northern Dallas and gentrifying Cedars/Downtown adjacent corridors
- Zero DPFC portfolio properties located in the 54 Susceptible South Dallas tracts
- Bishop Ridge development (Cedars): stacks OZ + Dallas PFC + Historic Tax Credits as explicit investor thesis

### Reform context
- HB 2071 (88th Regular Session, 2023): tightened affordability requirements, created reporting obligations
- Did NOT address geographic concentration or require location in disinvested communities
- October 2025: Dallas Housing and Homelessness Solutions Committee unanimously tabled proposed HFC/PFC program statement changes for further review

## Mechanism 2: Opportunity Zones (H8)

### Instrument design
OZs were created by the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, made permanent under 2025 tax legislation. Investors place capital gains into Qualified Opportunity Funds (QOFs) and receive: (1) capital gains tax deferral, (2) 15% reduction on owed gains after 7 years, (3) elimination of all taxes on appreciation after 10-year hold. Governors select up to 25% of eligible low-income census tracts for OZ designation.

### Who benefits — national data
- **$89 billion** flowed through OZ funds 2019–2022 (U.S. Treasury / NCRC 2025)
- **85% of OZ investors** are individuals with average annual incomes of **$4.9 million** — 99th percentile
- Average annual income of OZ benefit recipients in 2019 exceeded **$1 million** (JCT researcher, 2022)
- Only **16% of eligible tracts** received any OZ investment
- Top 1% of wealthiest OZ tracts received **42% of all investment**
- **75% of OZ funding** went to real estate, primarily market-rate residential rental
- **<3% of OZ-financed residential units** are explicitly affordable
- **69% of neighborhoods already gentrifying** 2013–2017 either contained or were adjacent to an OZ (NCRC 2025)

### Dallas pattern
- 15 OZ census tracts in Dallas; investment concentrated in Cedars, West Dallas, Oak Cliff Gateway, Lancaster Corridor
- **Zero OZ investment** in any of the 54 Susceptible South Dallas tracts
- **Bishop Ridge (Cedars):** ~$75M OZ equity across 36 separate QOFs in a single census tract; 23+ projects; 1,000+ units; stacks Dallas PFC + Historic Tax Credits
- **Newpark Dallas (Cedars):** ~$400M, 20-acre assemblage by Hoque Global; stacks ~$96.1M from Downtown Connection TIF + Public/Private Partnership Fund
- **Designation manipulation:** Hoque on record lobbying governor's office for OZ designation of land already owned — textbook example of designation capture by developers with political access

### Analytical framing
OZ in Dallas is not functioning as a capital redirection instrument. It is functioning as a **capital-gains shelter for gentrification that was already occurring**, with the designation process vulnerable to developer lobbying and the investment outcomes consistent with investor return maximization rather than community disinvestment reversal.

## Mechanism 3: CIP Procurement Geography (H9, in conjunction with H1/H2)

### The vendor residue dimension
The CIP allocation directs $984M (FY2012–2026) toward city physical infrastructure. The vendor residue analysis (H2/L5) shows that economic benefits of CIP expenditure do not remain in the communities where infrastructure is built:
- **South Dallas tracts** capture 2.6% ($38.3M) of vendor economic residue
- **North Dallas tracts** capture 25.3% ($369.2M) — **12.6× gap**
- Of $108.8M in CIP spending directed toward South Dallas, **83% of economic benefit flows northward** to firms headquartered in Northern Dallas or its suburbs

### BID Policy suspension
- City of Dallas Business Inclusion and Development (BID) Policy — in place since 2008 for minority/women-owned business participation — was **suspended June 2025** following EO 14173 (federal race/sex neutrality in contracting)
- Replacement: **DRIVE Framework** (Developing Regional & Inclusive Vendor Enterprises) — under development
- Policy vacuum in precisely the period when South Dallas development activity (OZ/PFC) is most intense

### Sunny South Dallas PID monitoring use case
- **Sunny South Dallas PID:** 1,375 parcels, $375.95M assessed value, $9M projected assessments over 10 years; Forest Forward as managing entity
- Double-edged instrument: genuine community self-determination mechanism, but previous South Dallas PID collapsed with financial accountability failures
- **Atlas monitoring function:** tract-level displacement risk + vendor residue tracking within PID boundary — independent monitoring framework that PID governance currently lacks
- Hypotheses H7+H8+H9 provide the evidential baseline against which PID outcomes can be measured

## The Stacked Inversion (H9)

The three mechanisms are individually justified as community benefit instruments and collectively function as a stacked inversion. The table below summarizes the extraction architecture:

| Mechanism | Stated Purpose | Actual Primary Beneficiary | Fiscal Cost Externalized To |
|---|---|---|---|
| PFC (up to 75-yr exemption) | Affordable multifamily housing | Northern-capitalized multifamily developers; LP investors | DISD tax base ($63.4M FSP increase/biennium); Dallas County homestead taxpayers |
| Opportunity Zones (capital-gains shelter) | Private investment in distressed communities | Top 1% investors (avg. $4.9M/yr); real estate syndicators | Federal deficit (→ HUD cuts); South Dallas legacy renters displaced by subsidized gentrification |
| CIP procurement concentration | Infrastructure investment in community | Northern Dallas contractor base; non-local vendors | South Dallas community wealth (12.6× residue gap; 83% of South CIP spend extracted northward) |

**Spatial output:** 40.1% of bottom-quintile capital-stack tracts sit south of I-30, against 28.7% baseline — a 1.4× overrepresentation of stacked disinvestment. The HOLC redlining coefficient β = +247.6 (p<0.001) is the strongest predictor of contemporary CIP allocation, with race becoming statistically non-significant when HOLC grade is controlled — meaning the stacked inversion is produced by the structural persistence of 1937 racial geography, not by any single contemporaneous discriminatory policy.

## Relationship to Five-Layer Framework

Layer 3b does not replace Layer 3 (TIF/OZ). It deepens it. The Layer 3 analysis identifies the **gap** (zero Susceptible South Dallas tracts with TIF or OZ investment). Layer 3b identifies the **mechanism** producing the gap: PFC exemptions that are structurally inaccessible to South Dallas community organizations; OZ investment that flows to the already-gentrifying corridors rather than the most vulnerable tracts; and CIP procurement that extracts economic multiplier effects northward even when infrastructure dollars are nominally directed south.

The combination of H7+H8+H9 constitutes the strongest version of the thesis argument: displacement in Dallas is not a market failure — it is a governance design. The fiscal architecture is producing it intentionally, in the sense that the structures contain no requirement to produce the community benefits they are publicly justified by referencing.
