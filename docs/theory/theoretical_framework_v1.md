## Theoretical Framework

Urban scholarship has long recognized that municipal investment decisions are not politically neutral. The allocation of public capital — through infrastructure bonds, tax capture mechanisms, and federal place-based incentives — reflects and reproduces the spatial hierarchies that historical policy first inscribed. This chapter situates the empirical analysis that follows within three intersecting bodies of theory: serial forced displacement as a cumulative spatial process, HOLC redlining as a causally documented mechanism of enduring wealth extraction, and the growth machine as the political-economic logic organizing contemporary investment geography. Taken together, these frameworks establish that Dallas's capital investment architecture — its CIP allocations, TIF districts, and Opportunity Zone designations — is not the product of neutral market forces or bureaucratic inertia. It is the contemporary expression of a governance system whose spatial logic was legible before a single bond was issued.

### Serial Forced Displacement and the Accumulation of Spatial Harm

The foundational premise of this thesis is that displacement is not a discrete event but an ongoing process whose harms compound across generations. Mindy Thompson Fullilove's concept of "root shock" — first elaborated in *Root Shock: How Tearing Up City Neighborhoods Hurts America and What We Can Do About It* (2004) — defines this condition as the traumatic stress reaction to the destruction of one's home environment, including the social networks, cultural institutions, and spatial memory that constitute neighborhood life. Fullilove documents how urban renewal, federally mandated highway construction, and systematic disinvestment operated serially on Black American communities: each wave of disruption found communities already weakened by the last, ensuring that recovery would be partial at best and that the next intervention would meet diminished resistance. The compounding nature of this process is its defining feature. Root shock is not an unfortunate side effect of development; it is an index of how governance structures organize spatial harm across time.

Wallace and Wallace's parallel work, *A Plague on Your Houses* (1998), provides the epidemiological complement to Fullilove's spatial framework. Analyzing the planned shrinkage of municipal fire services in the South Bronx, Wallace and Wallace demonstrate that disinvestment in public infrastructure produces cascading mortality, disease, and social disorganization — outcomes that, once set in motion, reshape the spatial substrate of future investment decisions. The community left by disinvestment becomes, in effect, less investable: its depleted social capital and degraded physical environment are then read by subsequent governance actors as evidence of intrinsic deficiency rather than as the legible output of prior policy choices.

This thesis reads Dallas's capital stack through this framework. The $485 million in CIP vendor payments documented north of I-30, compared with $38 million south of I-30 (H2 analysis), does not represent the first instance of South Dallas's exclusion from public capital flows. It represents a current chapter in a serial process whose earlier installments include the demolition of the Tenth Street Freedmen's Town grid, the routing of I-30 and I-35 as racial boundary infrastructure, and the systematic exclusion of South Dallas neighborhoods from urban renewal funds that were, in the wider national pattern, themselves instruments of clearance and community destruction (Rothstein 2017). The Bates (2013) typology's prospective dimension — identifying neighborhoods where the conditions for displacement are currently staged rather than already executed — captures precisely the serial logic Fullilove theorizes: displacement is already under construction in communities that have not yet been moved.

### Redlining as Causal Mechanism: From Historical Inscription to Contemporary Manifestation

The second theoretical anchor is the growing body of causal scholarship establishing that HOLC-era redlining produced effects on neighborhood wealth, credit access, and racial composition that remain empirically detectable in the present. Aaronson, Hartley, and Mazumder's landmark study in the *American Economic Journal: Economic Policy* (2021) advances the causal claim most rigorously. Their regression discontinuity design exploits geographic discontinuities at HOLC grade boundaries — comparing tracts that received adjacent grades (B versus C, or C versus D) — to isolate the effect of the grade assignment itself from underlying neighborhood characteristics that may have influenced grading. The identification strategy is consequential: it rules out the possibility that redlined neighborhoods are simply poorer or Blacker today because they were already poorer or Blacker in 1940. The grade assignment, Aaronson et al. find, was itself a treatment that depressed subsequent homeownership rates, constrained intergenerational wealth accumulation, and restricted credit access in ways that remained statistically significant eight decades later.

This thesis takes the Aaronson et al. causal framework as a spatial warrant for reading present-day data as downstream evidence of an identified historical mechanism rather than as independent observations requiring separate explanation. The HMDA 2023 denial rate ratio of 1.19× (South/North Dallas) and the Black share decomposition of 20.7% in mortgage denial excess are not anomalies demanding their own causal stories; they are contemporary manifestations of the credit restriction mechanism that Aaronson et al. document as causally traceable to HOLC grading. Rothstein's (2017) historical reconstruction of the legal architecture of segregation in *The Color of Law* supplies the institutional detail — the FHA underwriting manuals, the racially restrictive covenant enforcement, the exclusion of Black borrowers from the VA mortgage program — that connects the HOLC map to the lending patterns documented in HMDA a generation removed.

Dantzler (2021) extends this lineage into the present through the framework of racial capitalism and urban process, arguing that the urban built environment is not incidentally racialized but constitutively so: the production of urban space under capitalism requires the devaluation of Black-occupied land as a precondition for the extraction of value from adjacent or successor development. This thesis's capital stack model is consistent with Dantzler's framework. The 26:1 TIF increment ratio — Downtown Connection at $8.83 billion versus Grand Park South at $333 million — is not a market discovery about the relative productivity of these geographies. It is a socially produced ratio whose terms were set by a century of governance decisions that first cleared, then contained, and now strategically reinvests around communities of color south of I-30.

### The Growth Machine and the Political Economy of Spatial Investment

The third framework situates the capital stack within the political economy of urban governance. Logan and Molotch's *Urban Fortunes: The Political Economy of Place* (1987) establishes the foundational tension in urban land markets between use value — the experiential, social, and material value a place holds for its residents — and exchange value — its worth as a commodity in capital markets. The growth machine is the political coalition — comprising municipal governments, real estate interests, financial institutions, major employers, and utilities — organized to maximize the exchange value of urban space, and to do so by mobilizing public policy as an instrument of capital accumulation. Growth machine theory predicts that public investment will concentrate where it amplifies private returns, and will be withheld from communities whose exchange value cannot be profitably leveraged by growth coalition members.

Weber's (2002) analysis of TIF as a technology of neoliberal urban redevelopment, published in *Antipode*, provides the specific mechanism through which growth machine logic operates in the Dallas context. Weber demonstrates that TIF is not simply a development subsidy; it is a financial engineering device that redirects future public tax revenues away from general obligation uses — schools, parks, public health infrastructure — and toward the private development projects of growth coalition participants, on the rationale that the increment in assessed value would not have occurred without the public subsidy. The "but for" standard, Weber shows, is almost never seriously evaluated and is routinely satisfied by political assertion. The practical consequence is that TIF is a mechanism for converting public fiscal capacity into private returns, with the spatial targeting of that conversion reflecting the exchange-value priorities of the governing coalition. In Dallas, the 18 active TIF districts and their combined $27.2 billion in assessed value are not distributed across the city's geography according to need; they are distributed according to the growth machine's legible investment logic, which systematically excludes areas whose exchange value has been suppressed — by prior policy — to levels that do not meet the coalition's threshold for activation.

Wyly and Hammel (2004) bring gentrification into direct dialogue with segregation and TIF, documenting how tax capture mechanisms can function as instruments of displacement even in the absence of explicit racial targeting, because the spatial geography of disinvestment that TIF inherits is itself racially structured. The expansion of TIF-funded development into communities adjacent to historically redlined areas operates along the grain of the racial geography already inscribed, amplifying the displacement pressure on communities of color whose neighborhoods represent the last low-cost parcels proximate to growth coalition investment zones.

### Theoretical Contribution

This thesis advances an original theoretical contribution that none of the three bodies of literature reviewed above yet provides. Serial displacement theory establishes the cumulative logic of harm but has not been operationalized at the tract level using a decomposed capital stack. Causal redlining scholarship establishes the historical mechanism but does not extend its spatial analysis to the contemporary governance instruments — CIP, TIF, OZ — through which that mechanism continues to operate. Growth machine theory provides the political-economic logic but has not been applied, in a single integrated analysis, to the full vertical architecture of capital flows in a Sun Belt city using primary HMDA, CIP vendor payment, and TIF district data. This thesis supplies that integration. By decomposing the capital stack into five interlocking layers — municipal CIP, private supplemental assessment (PID), TIF/OZ financial engineering, the mortgage lending market, and institutional ownership concentration — and mapping each layer to tract-level geographies, this thesis operationalizes investability not as an objective property of neighborhoods but as a constructed condition produced by racially structured governance. The Bates Typology v2.1 longitudinal classification then adds the prospective dimension that existing scholarship lacks: not only where disinvestment has occurred, but where the serial displacement machinery is currently staged. The result is a spatial political economy of investment in Dallas that is simultaneously a test of Aaronson et al.'s causal framework in a new urban context, an application of growth machine theory to a previously undocumented capital architecture, and a documentation of the mechanism by which root shock is currently being administered to South Dallas communities south of I-30. The framework is not descriptive; it is diagnostic.
